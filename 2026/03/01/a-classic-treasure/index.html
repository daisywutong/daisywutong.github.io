The Classic Weapon

Today's world is like a giant maze of violence, turmoil, and indifference, with every person searching for a way out. But what if a solution to some of our most serious issues already exists—one with little to no cost and countless benefits for the human mind? The answer lies in classic literature. Far from being mere entertainment, these works are powerful tools for personal growth and intellectual enrichment. Classic literature comprises written works that have stood the test of time and are considered exemplary in their craft and insight. But what makes classic literature the ultimate weapon against the current problems is its ability to challenge our thinking, deepen our understanding of the world, and cultivate empathy.

One of the most pressing issues today is the prevalence of intellectual conformity, often fueled by social media platforms that create "echo chambers." These platforms show users information that aligns with their existing beliefs, meaning that many people may never be exposed to different opinions. This situation is similar to the dystopian society in George Orwell's 1984, where citizens only receive information controlled by the government, which may make us aware of the importance of critical thinking and source checking when searching for information.

Additionally, our lives have been colonized by short videos and social media, resulting in many people prioritizing fleeting pleasure over deep thinking and genuine fulfillment, leaving them too distracted to engage meaningfully with the world. Toward this issue, classic literature has offered two strong responses. One is Antoine de Saint-Exupéry's book The Little Prince. Through the journey with the little prince across the universe, readers may learn to look beyond money, wealth and fame or any of the superficial comforts, but to understand what truly matters in life, such as love and responsibility. They may see the world from a simpler but more meaningful perspective.

Another is Aldous Huxley's novel Brave New World. Unlike the totalitarian society in 1984 where the government has to control the people through harsh laws and propaganda, citizens in Brave New World are not forced to give up their freedom—they simply choose pleasure over thinking, distracted by entertainment and kept satisfied by a drug called soma, which is terrifyingly similar to our real lives. Reading Brave New World may help us realize that the greatest danger to our freedom is not always force or punishment, but our own willingness to trade deep thinking for conformity, which is a decision we should never make.

Moreover, it is very common for people to judge others quickly based on a simple label, such as their appearance or background, without truly trying to understand them. On the internet, this kind of behavior can easily turn into bullying, where a large group of people attacks someone they know very little about. This issue is the exact reason why we should read the book To Kill a Mockingbird, which is considered an all-time classic for the beliefs it sends. The story is set in a society with strong racial prejudice, where a black man Tom Robinson is unfairly accused. Atticus Finch, the father in the book, chooses to defend Tom Robinson, even though many people oppose his decision. The book reveals the serious problem of social prejudice and racial discrimination. It may also illustrate the value of courage, justice and the importance of not losing conscience under mainstream opinions. When confronting Boo Radley, Atticus taught his children to respect and understand the town freak despite his infamous reputation, because he believed that "You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view… until you climb into his skin and walk around in it", which may encourage teenagers not to fear or discriminate, but to show empathy and compassion to people who are different or peculiar. In this way, classic literature like To Kill a Mockingbird may educate readers into becoming brave, responsible and compassionate social members.

In conclusion, classic literature is far more than old stories that may pleasure us. Instead, they, with their power to challenge our minds, awaken our consciences, and cultivate our empathy, are the maps that may help us survive in the never-ending "maze" of flooded information and little wisdom.
