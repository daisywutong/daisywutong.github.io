A Classic Treasure

What if there was a way to travel around the world without stepping outside, to uncover hidden mysteries without a Time Machine, and to gain the wisdom of ancient sages at little cost? The answer lies in classic literature. Far from being mere entertainment, these works are powerful tools for personal growth and intellectual enrichment. According to Longman Dictionary of Contemporary English, classic refers to "admired by many people, and having a value that has continued for a long time", while literature means "books, plays, poems that are important and good". So, by combining these two definitions, classic literature stands for written works that have stood the test of time and are considered exemplary. But what makes classic literature valuable is its ability to enhance a person's sense of beauty, to broaden one's horizon and teach important life lessons.

First of all, classic literature may also help people appreciate and understand beauty. For instance, in "He Wishes for the Cloths of Heaven", Yeats uses metaphor to compare his love with "the heavens' embroidered cloths", which may suggest that his love is precious, sacred and beautiful. The poem also uses rich visual imagery such as "golden and silver light" and "blue and dark cloths", creating a dreamlike, magical atmosphere. Then the poet contrasts "the heavens' embroidered cloths" with his own humble dreams, which may indicate the fragility and deep value of his love. By enjoying the artistic language, readers may gain aesthetic perception and understand the value of classic literature. As the film Dead Poets Society points out: "poetry, beauty, romance, love — these are what we stay alive for." This quote states the importance in understanding beauty and human emotions, which is also the true value of classic literature to readers.

Secondly, classic literature may also broaden readers' perspectives by presenting complex problems and social issues. One famous example is George Orwell's dystopian novel 1984. The story depicts a totalitarian world where the government controls people's thoughts and freedom, which may show readers the danger of unlimited power and help them view society more critically. Aldous Huxley's Brave New World offers another form of controlled society, but in a completely different style. In the dystopian society in Brave New World, the citizens are controlled by science and technology, meaning that they seem satisfied outside but never experience any true feelings or develop any independent thoughts. This book may remind readers not to trade true emotions and critical thinking for happiness and conformity, especially under today's society, which is flooded with entertainment products.

Another note-worthy value of classic literature is that it teaches readers moral values. For example, take the book To Kill a Mockingbird, which is considered an all-time classic for the beliefs it conveys. The story is set in a society with strong racial prejudice, where a black man Tom Robinson is unfairly accused. Atticus Finch, the father in the book, chooses to defend Tom Robinson, even though many people oppose his decision. The book reveals the serious problem of social prejudice and racial discrimination. It may also illustrate the value of courage, justice and the importance of not losing conscience under mainstream opinions. When confronting Boo Radley, Atticus taught his children to respect and understand the town recluse despite his infamous reputation, because he believed that "You never really understand a person until you consider things from his point of view… until you climb into his skin and walk around in it", which may encourage teenagers not to fear or discriminate, but to show empathy and compassion to people who are different or peculiar. In this way, classic literature like To Kill a Mockingbird may educate readers into brave, responsible and compassionate social members.

In conclusion, classic literature is far more than old stories that may pleasure us. They may cultivate our aesthetic awareness, broaden our horizons on social issues, and help us form positive moral values. In the present world, classic works may retain their importance as they guide us to think, feel and live in a more meaningful way.
